CATFISH BLUES

a Zine by Brooke Ryan


The object of circulation I have chosen is the song “Catfish Blues.” A traditional blues song that originated in the Mississippi Delta region with the first known recording from 1941 by blues musician Robert Petway, the song has been circulated through several genres of music and periods of time. While the musical style in which “Catfish Blues” is played has varied, the preservation of the song from the traditional genre of Mississippi Delta Blues which arose as a result of systematic oppression under the Black Codes, makes the song a Circulation of Blackness. One trans/national context in which this song can be considered is during the Great Migration period, specifically during the 1940s when blues music was also being brought to the northern states as a result of fleeing violence from terrorist organizations in the south. As discussed in lecture, the illegality of slavery in Northern states caused the region to be systematically designated as “anti-black” states. Bringing blues music from the south to the Northern states was in itself an act of defiance and preservation of blackness and black culture. Muddy Waters utilized lines from “Catfish” for the song “Rollin’ Stone.” Another trans/national context in which “Catfish” has circulated is during 1968. Jimi Hendrix adapted lines from “Catfish Blues” for the song “Voodoo Chile.” Jimi Hendrix performed the song at Woodstock music festival.
